A B S T R A C T
There have been recent efforts to use reality capture technologies to perform remote
quality control in construction. However, there is a lack of research efforts in
detecting construction incompatibilities in modular construction using reality
capture technologies. The construction incompatibilities in modular construction
often cause reworks and delays in the project schedule. To address this issue, this
paper presents a general compatibility analysis method that propose scanning the
modules in manufacturing plant and construction site, and check module-to-module
compatibility remotely, prior to the shipment and installation. This study provides
three sample module-to-module compatibility scenarios to validate the proposed
compatibility analysis. The case study results show that the compatibility analysis
method was able to identify the compatibility issues with high accuracy. Lastly, the
compatibility analysis method was validated in terms of accuracy and time
performance in six scenarios that was defined on the modules.

Despite these benefits, offsite manufacturing often introduces significant challenges that need to be18

whole project [9,10]. Modules often need to be modified on-site to fix the incompatibilities, which increase20
rework and introduce new challenges. If modules are not repairable on-site, remanufacturing and shipping will21
lead to even greater delays and cost overruns. To avoid rework and remanufacturing, researchers suggested22

approach, geometric defects can be identified at the manufacturing facility before shipment. Researchers have25

and industrial modules [11].27
The main limitation of the current methods in detecting the geometric defects is that the modules are28
investigated individually. However, the defects often occur in module-to-module incompatibilities, especially29
when there is a discrepancy or error in the design model versus the as-built. For example, a piping module may30
not be compatible with its connecting modules due to changing site conditions even if each module meets the31
required geometric standards. To address this limitation, this paper presents a compatibility checking32

framework. In this framework, modules in the manufacturing facilities and the main structure on the project site33
are scanned. The presented semi-automated compatibility assessment method detects module-to-module34

Previous research efforts are only focused on the quality assessment of a single module or component36

are two factors that can adversely affect the accuracy of the proposed quality assessments methods [20]. The45
presented method in the following section attempts to address these gaps in knowledge.46
The main contributions of this study are 1) a compatibility assessment method that checks compatibility47
between two modules and quantifies the gap between them, ensuring quality of fabricated components before48
they are shipped and assembled and 2) a generalized method that work with different types and shapes of49
modular/fabricated components. This compatibility method was tested and validated using three types of offsite50
manufactured modules with varying shapes.51
2 Background52
Over the past few years, reality capturing technologies have received significant popularity in the AEC industry,53
namely construction progress and performance monitoring [21–23], assembly training [15], construction54
quantity takeoff [24], safety [25], and inspection [23,26]. However, there are still numerous research questions55
to be investigated.56
Module misplacement has always been a challenge in construction. Research studies wanted to avoid this57
by employing new technologies that can automatically detect the misplacement [27]. Researchers proposed58

AUTOMATION IN CONSTRUCTION 00 (2020) 000–000 4
using point clouds to detect module position mismatch based on scan-to-BIM registrations in pipe spools [28],59
however this method works only on uncluttered point clouds [29]. Beside point clouds, researchers suggested60
using video surveillance to detect the position of precast concrete modules [30]. Also, researchers proposed a61
technique that can accurately estimate rebar positions on reinforced precast concrete bridge deck panels [19].62
Researchers presents an algorithm for automated discrepancy quantification of construction components63
[29,31]. The common limitation of all of these studies was that their system only works for a single module64
under a certain condition and the proposed method could not be used on wide variety of modules [29,31].65
In addition to the modules position, module dimension is another important factor. Researchers proposed66
automated systems that uses scan-to-BIM registration to perform dimensional quality assessment of precast67
concrete elements [32,33]. Similar methods performed quality assessments on the concrete steel embedded68
plates [34]. Researchers expanded the methods to measure rebar sizes [14] and module dimensions [35].69
Ultimately, researchers focused on detected module defects such as warping in precast concrete modules [33]70
and deflection of the bridges for maintenance [36]. They suggested using octree and voxelization to measure the71
bridge deflections [36]. However, their suggested method is only applicable to the bridge maintenance and72
could not be used on various construction elements [36]. Also, the researchers developed methods to73
automatically detect the squareness of shear keys in a precast concrete module [13].74
Their main limitation was that their method was only applicable on small size modules. Lastly, researchers75
suggested geometric quality inspection technique to detect defects for prefabricated MEP modules [11] and a76
framework for surface quality assessment of precast concrete elements using edge extraction algorithms [32,33].77

places – 1) where a module of interest is manufactured and 2) where this module is shipped to and assembled.99
To generate the 3D as-built models of the module and its connecting part (i.e., building component), a reliable100

3.2 Data registration104
The registration process can use any existing registration method (i.e., automated [38], semi-automated [39,40],105
or manual). For this study, a semi-automated registration was performed similar to [39,40]. Six corresponding106
points (features or markers) were selected from both BIM and the point cloud to register the point cloud to the107
BIM. Through this registration, the two modules’ point clouds are appropriately positioned for a compatibility108
check. This step is necessary since checking the module-to-module compatibility is not possible without placing109
the point clouds to their installation location. The corresponding points were used to solve the least square110
registration problem of absolute orientation for seven degrees of freedom [41], which returns a transformation111
matrix and a registration error. The transformation matrix is used to perform a 3D linear transformation [42,43].112
In case of a high registration error, the registration has to be iterated until the accuracy of the registration113
reaches below the user requirement/threshold. Also, other methods can be used for registration including114
automated registration using fiduciary markers and surveying coordinates. In this research, we only focus on the115
module-to-module interaction, and we assume that the elements already passed the dimensional qualities with116
respect to their BIM models. Several past studies suggested methods to compare BIM and Scan models [15,44].117

5 Discussion and future works291
The proposed compatibility checking method can be used in several domains as follows. The first domain can292
be permitting operations. For example, in the case of concrete pipe installation, the gap between modules should293
not exceed a certain threshold based on local codes. The current methods for performing such operations can be294
done only after the modules (e.g., pipes) have been shipped and installed in place. Such procedure can often295
cause rework in cases where the required threshold is not met. The second domain is where the modules does296
not fit in place which can cause delays and rework to the project. For example, if the rebars do not fit in the297
holes in precast concrete modules, the compatibility analysis can easily detect that.298
One of the strengths of this method is that any scanning device/technology that meets the user's quality299
requirement can be used. On the other hand, the main limitation of the proposed system is that the data300

collection process (i.e., use of terrestrial scanners) is often time consuming. In future there is a need for devices301
that can scan components at a much faster rate. Also, noise can adversely affect the accuracy of the proposed302
method. Therefore, there is a need for research efforts that can minimize the amount of noise in the point cloud303
especially for metallic objects (or any with reflective surfaces) as metal is one of the commonly used materials304
in the construction. Furthermore, the processing time of the compatibility analysis is directly correlated with the305
number of points in the modules. Therefore, there is a need for methods that can sample point clouds effectively306
and reduce the processing time especially for the point clouds that are generated with high precision hand-held307
laser scanners. Lastly, the proposed compatibility checking system presented high performance on the308

The compatibility algorithm has several limitations that need to be addressed. Firstly, having high level of noise321
in the scanned model point cloud could potentially fail the proposed compatibility algorithm. For example, this322

algorithm could not be used for point clouds that are generated using photogrammetry. Also, the objects that are323
transparent or metallic might introduce extensive amount of noise in the scanned model and cause the algorithm324
to fail. Moreover, several steps involve manual processes, such as registration, selection of cross-section plane,325
and offset for compatibility. These manual steps could be automated by integrating automated registration326
methods similar to [15,44] and methods to select the compatibility plane and offset using learning-based method327
that imitate human agent.328
6 Conclusion329
Over the past few years, reality capture technologies have received significant popularity in the AEC industry,330
namely construction progress monitoring [21], assembly training [15], construction quantity takeoff [24], safety331
[25,48], and inspection [23]. However, there are still numerous research questions to be investigated, such as332
efficient and general compatibility checking of offsite components. To address this issue and improve the333
compatibility checking process, this paper presents a generalized method for compatibility checking of334
fabricated components. The proposed method is a powerful tool in detecting geometric defects and335
incompatibilities between modules in modular construction. This compatibility system is robust to occlusions,336
noise, and can be applied on various types of point clouds that are captured using different approaches and337
devices. The system was tested and validated in three different scenarios and demonstrated the effectiveness and338
robustness of the proposed method for compatibility analysis on the as-built elements and verified the339
compatibility of the as-built models.340
